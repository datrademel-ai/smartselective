What's Tested

Numerical Reasoning assesses your child's ability to recognize patterns, interpret data, and solve quantitative problems using logic rather than complex calculations. It tests analytical thinking and the ability to work with numbers, sequences, and visual patterns.

Question Types

  • • Number sequences: Find the pattern and next number (e.g., 2, 4, 8, 16, __)
  • • Visual patterns: Identify which shape comes next in a sequence
  • • Data interpretation: Read graphs, charts, or tables to answer questions
  • • Logic problems: Use given information to deduce answers

Study Focus - What to Practice

  • ✓ Pattern recognition: Practice identifying arithmetic, geometric, and Fibonacci sequences
  • ✓ Mental math: Quick addition, subtraction, multiplication, division without calculator
  • ✓ Graph reading: Interpret bar charts, line graphs, pie charts, and tables
  • ✓ Logical thinking: Solve puzzles that require deductive reasoning

Time Management Tips

  • • Spend no more than 45-50 seconds per question
  • • Skip difficult questions and return to them if time permits
  • • Check your work if you finish early
  • • Don't get stuck on one pattern - move on and come back

What's Tested

Reading Comprehension evaluates your child's ability to understand, analyze, and interpret written texts. It tests literal comprehension (what the text says), inferential understanding (reading between the lines), and critical analysis (evaluating the author's purpose and technique).

Passage Types

  • • Fiction: Short stories, novel excerpts (character, plot, theme analysis)
  • • Non-fiction: Articles, reports, informational texts (main idea, supporting details)
  • • Poetry: Poems (imagery, mood, figurative language)
  • • Persuasive: Opinion pieces, arguments (author's viewpoint, evidence)

Study Focus - What to Practice

  • ✓ Wide reading: Read diverse materials - fiction, news articles, science texts, poetry
  • ✓ Vocabulary building: Learn new words in context, understand synonyms and antonyms
  • ✓ Inference skills: Practice reading between the lines - what's implied but not stated
  • ✓ Main idea identification: Summarize passages in one sentence
  • ✓ Author's purpose: Identify why the author wrote the text (inform, persuade, entertain)

Reading Strategy Tips

  • • Read the questions BEFORE reading the passage (know what to look for)
  • • Underline or highlight key information as you read
  • • Refer back to the text - don't rely on memory alone
  • • Eliminate obviously wrong answers first
  • • Watch for "extreme" words in answer choices (always, never, must) - usually incorrect

What's Tested

Mathematics tests mathematical reasoning, problem-solving ability, and computational skills. Questions assess understanding of mathematical concepts and the ability to apply them to real-world scenarios. The focus is on thinking mathematically, not just memorizing formulas.

Key Topics Covered

  • • Number & Algebra: Fractions, decimals, percentages, ratios, basic algebra
  • • Geometry: Shapes, angles, area, perimeter, volume, symmetry
  • • Measurement: Time, distance, mass, capacity, unit conversions
  • • Statistics: Mean, median, mode, range, basic probability
  • • Word Problems: Multi-step problems requiring logical thinking

Study Focus - What to Practice

  • ✓ Mental arithmetic: Fast calculation without calculator - times tables to 12×12
  • ✓ Fraction operations: Adding, subtracting, multiplying, dividing fractions
  • ✓ Percentage calculations: Finding percentages, percentage increase/decrease
  • ✓ Problem-solving strategies: Breaking complex problems into steps
  • ✓ Formula application: Area, perimeter, volume formulas for common shapes

Problem-Solving Tips

  • • Read the question carefully - highlight what you're asked to find
  • • Show your working (helps catch errors and partial credit may be given)
  • • Check if your answer makes sense (e.g., is it reasonable?)
  • • Use estimation to quickly verify answers
  • • Don't spend too long on one question - move on and return if needed

What's Tested

Verbal Reasoning assesses your child's ability to think logically about words and language. It tests understanding of word relationships, analogies, classifications, and logical sequences. Strong vocabulary and the ability to see patterns in language are essential.

Question Types

  • • Analogies: A is to B as C is to __? (e.g., hot:cold :: up:__)
  • • Word relationships: Find the odd one out or identify common connections
  • • Synonyms/Antonyms: Identify words with similar or opposite meanings
  • • Letter sequences: Complete patterns based on alphabetical logic
  • • Code breaking: Decipher patterns in letter/number substitutions

Study Focus - What to Practice

  • ✓ Vocabulary expansion: Learn 5-10 new words daily with meanings and usage
  • ✓ Word relationships: Practice identifying how words relate (synonyms, categories, part-whole)
  • ✓ Analogy practice: Work through analogy problems to recognize common patterns
  • ✓ Word games: Crosswords, word searches, Scrabble build vocabulary naturally
  • ✓ Prefixes/suffixes: Understanding word parts helps decode unfamiliar words

Vocabulary Building Tips

  • • Read widely - exposure to new words in context is most effective
  • • Keep a vocabulary notebook - write new words with definitions and example sentences
  • • Learn word roots, prefixes, and suffixes (e.g., "bi-" means two)
  • • Use new words in conversation and writing to reinforce learning
  • • Group words by theme or relationship (e.g., emotions: happy, ecstatic, joyful)
